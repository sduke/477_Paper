What is DH?

Digital Humanities: the new frontier to humanities research. Research projects cover a broad spectrum of concepts, ranging from three-dimensional reconstructions of historical sites, like Virtual Williamsburg or Digital Augustan Rome, to literary archives containing the works, correspondences, and interviews of some of history's most prominent authors, such as The Walt Whitman Archive and The Willa Cather Archive.

But what exactly is digital humanities? A simple Google search will give you a multitude of definitions that imply different things. The website whatisdigitalhumanities.com brings up a different definition every time you refresh the page: “David Wacks: Anything a Humanities scholar does that is mediated digitally, especially when such meditation opens discussion beyond a small circle of academic specialists.” “Alice Hickcox: Humanists creating, using, researching digital materials to do their work.” “Wikipedia: Digital humanities (DH) is an area of scholarly activity at the intersection of computing and the disciplines of the humanities.” However, DH projects, in and of themselves, are working programs that seek to discover new meaning, or a better understanding, in text, print, and other humanities through digital means.

Because DH is its own interdisciplinary field, drawing from English, history, and other various humanities specialties, it establishes its own literacy. In practice, DH involves encoding, scanning, mapping, formatting, and database development. And that is just the digital side of things. The humanities side goes further into reading and understanding a text, or series of texts, or the like, as well as attempting to look at the corpus from several different potential viewpoints.

Digital humanities is an up and coming field in academia. Colleges across the United States and the world are developing programs central to DH research. As defined by Matthew Kirschenbaum in his article “What is Digital Humanities and What’s It Doing in English Departments?”, digital humanities “is about a scholarship (and a pedagogy) that is publicly visible in ways to which we are generally unaccustomed, a scholarship and pedagogy that are bound up with infrastructure in ways that are deeper and more explicit than we are generally accustomed to, a scholarship and pedagogy that are collaborative and depend on networks of people and that live an active 24/7 life online.”
